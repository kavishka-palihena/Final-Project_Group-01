Quality Characteristics of Water

Ensuring access to clean and safe drinking water is essential for human health and well-being. Let's explore the key parameters that define water quality:

  1. pH value: Indicates the acidity or alkalinity level of water. The optimal range recommended by WHO is 6.5 - 8.5.
  2. Hardness: Measures the presence of calcium and magnesium salts in water. Excessive hardness can lead to scale buildup in pipes and appliances.
  3. Solids (Total dissolved solids - TDS): Reflects the mineral content dissolved in water. Higher TDS levels may affect taste, odor, and appearance.
  4. Chloramines: Common disinfectants used in water treatment to eliminate harmful pathogens and maintain water quality.
  5. Sulfate: Naturally occurring substances found in minerals. Elevated sulfate levels may indicate contamination from industrial or agricultural activities.
  6. Conductivity: Measures water's ability to conduct electricity. Elevated conductivity may suggest increased mineral content or pollution.
  7. Organic carbon: Indicates the presence of organic compounds in water, which can impact odor, taste, and color.
  8. Trihalomethanes: Chemicals formed during water chlorination. Prolonged exposure to high levels may pose health risks, including cancer.
  9. Turbidity: Measures the clarity of water by assessing suspended solids. Higher turbidity levels may indicate contamination from sediment, algae, or other particles.
  10. Potability: Determines if water is safe for consumption. Always adhere to safety guidelines and regulatory standards.

Please note: Regular monitoring and adherence to water quality standards are essential for ensuring public health and safety.
